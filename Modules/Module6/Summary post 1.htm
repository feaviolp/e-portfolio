Agent Based Systems - Summary Post

In my initial post I stated that the rise of Agent Based Systems (ABS), like other innovations, is driven by opportunities for organisational financial gain Taalbi (2017). It wasn’t entirely superficial, and was intended to give a broader context which Adeniyi (2023a) found insightful.

I then gave examples in Supply Chain Management (SCM), from Azevedo et al (2004) where agents are deployed to manage a supply chain, and from Nilsson & Darley (2006) where agents are used to model a supply chain. At the time I didn’t consider the difference, but upon reading the other posts I noticed that van Toor (2023a), Kanakavelu (2023) and Rizzo (2023) each gave similar examples of agents that operate in our physical world and agents that model in an artificial world, which I termed as “active” and modelling” agents respectively to differentiate them in one of my replies (Feaviour, 2023a).

Van Toor (2023b) made some excellent observations about legacy integration, noting the challenge of integrating with legacy systems (Bennett, 1995) and one solution being to use ABS to encapsulate legacy systems with middleware approaches (Shen, 2006). Since many SCM systems are old, and not trivial to replace because they are directly integrated with their physical environment, this seems like an excellent additional use ABS, using agents as containers to enable other “active” agents to be deployed.

Adeniyi (2023b) noted that ABS are able to work with incomplete information (Monostori, 2019). I mentioned in my reply (Feaviour, 2023b) that with the ever increasing amount of data available, partly driven by the explosion of IoT devices, ABS are therefore likely to continue to feature heavily in systems architecture.

The discussion was interesting, covering many different industries and use cases. Considering the arguments, I believe that the use of ABS will continue to grow.
