Initial Post

Innovation is driven by problems and opportunities. Sometimes the problems are known and we look for creative ways to solve them. Sometimes we see the potential of a new technology and then we look for opportunities to leverage it. In most cases the overriding drive is financial.

Taalbi (2017: 1449) sums it up nicely, that “most innovations […] were developed as a creative response to problems and imbalances emerging in the process of economic development or spurred by the observation of new technological opportunities.”

So the superficial answer to the question of what has led to the rise of agent-based systems would seem to be “for organisations to exploit the benefits for financial gain”. That’s not particularly helpful though, so lets look at some examples in the supply chain industry.

Dominguez & Cannella (2020) carried out a literature review of the applications of multi-agent systems on supply chain management. They identified many areas of supply chain management benefiting to varying degrees from agent-based systems so we will look at two.

Azevedo et al (2004) describes a multi-agent system for real-time make-to-order customer order planning when the fulfilment requires multiple distributed sites. Agents are deployed within each site to plan and manage the capacity at that site in collaboration with planner agents at each site who negotiate with their counterparts at the other sites to ensure that the plans meet the overall customer objectives. In other words, there is no point in site A delivering on time if it is dependent upon site B which delivers late. The customer is only happy if all requirements are met on time. The planner agents have the view of the customer requirements and negotiate to ensure that each site delivers what is needed using feedback from the capacity agents across all sites relevant for a given customer order. This is not possible with conventional software, so the agent-based system improves capacity planning at individual sites (a cost saving) whilst improving cashflow from on-time delivery and future revenue through increased customer satisfaction.

A slightly different use case is presented in Nilsson & Darley (2006) where agents are used not to control a factory, but to model it. Here the factory was modelled with agents autonomously representing machines, sales, operations planning, the warehouse and customers. The model could be used to test scenarios such as the introduction of new policies or machines or the growth or loss of major customers. One key benefit of the multi-agent approach, in addition to providing greater accuracy, was that it made both macro and micro analysis possible. As well as monitoring the behaviour of the factory, individual agents could also be monitored and evaluated, meaning that operations could be optimised on the model before being implemented in the more costly “real world”.

 

References

Nilsson, F. & Darley, V. (2006) On complex adaptive systems and agent‐based modelling for improving decision‐making in manufacturing and logistics settings: Experiences from a packaging company. International Journal of Operations & Production Management, 26(12): 1351-1373.
