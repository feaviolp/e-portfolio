Codes of Ethics and Professional Conduct - Summary Post

In my initial post I provided a fairly sterile comparison of compliance of the Blocker Plus case study (Association for Computing Machinery, N.D.) with ACM Code of Ethics and Professional Conduct (Association for Computing Machinery, 2018), referred to as “ACM”, and the Code of Conduct for BCS Members (British Computer Society, 2022), referred to as “BCS”. The two codes were found to be similar, but with some key gaps, and generally ACM was more thorough than BCS.

The follow-up discussion was useful in providing more real-world angles to the analysis. Particularly interesting was the discussion about the fact that neither code of conduct is legally binding (Lambert, 2023), which calls into question their real value. I maintained when discussing the Automated Active Response Weaponry case study, however, that even though they are not legally binding, a set of ethical standards still has value (Feaviour, 2023a). We both agreed that the whistle-blowers, which believing they were behaving ethically, were certainly not behaving lawfully (Lambert, 2023), (Feaviour, 2023a).

Another useful discussion concerned how culpable Blocker Plus should be for failing to foresee and protect against activists from corrupting the system. Lambert (2023) argued that it is unreasonable to expect any system to be completely secure, which I agree with, however I believe that Blocker Plus could and should have predicted some form of misuse by bad actors, and there was evidence that they had insufficient protection designed into the system or the support processes (Feaviour, 2023b). Raneli (2023) agreed, saying “Blocker Plus's failure to preemptively secure its model and the subsequent difficulty in rectification suggest a deviation from these ethical expectations”.

In response to a review of Dark UX Patterns (Castro, 2023), I questioned how accountable a development agency or an individual developer should be when asked by a customer to do something unethical (Feaviour, 2023c). Bandy (2023) felt that both the developer and development agency had a duty to adhere to the principles and act ethically. I agree, but I also understand that the developer in particular would feel pressure from the agency and would need to weight up his own ethical principles against his desire to keep a job.

In summary, the ethical principles provide useful ethical guidance, and the seem to be fairly binary in their interpretation. However, they operate without legal recourse and within environments where context is important.
