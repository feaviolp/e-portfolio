Knowledge Representation is a recent phenomenon - Initial Post

Kalibatiene & Vasilecas (2011) actually took their definition that “An ontology is a formal, explicit specification of a shared conceptualization” from Gruber (1993).

Knowledge Interchange Format (KIF) is highly expressive, using first-order logic, but that can lead to heavyweight systems (Kalibatiene & Vasilecas, 2011), although Pulido et al. (2006) suggest KIF is “computationally attractive”.

Resource Description Framework (RDF) is less expressive than KIF and uses an object-oriented structure to define resources with properties and values, with each having a Uniform Resource Identifier (URI) (Kalibatiene & Vasilecas, 2011). It also has a lot of tools to support it.

Web Ontology Language (OWL) is in its second generation; OWL2, which has three variations; Owl-Lite, OWL-DL and OWL-Full. It is built on RDF and was designed for the semantic web (Kalibatiene & Vasilecas, 2011).

OWL-Lite is the least expressive, followed by OWL-DL and then OWL-Full as the most expressive (Kalibatiene & Vasilecas, 2011). However, the lack of “restrictions on the use of transitive properties” in OWL-Full mean that inference is undecidable (Horrocks, 2005: 6).

OWL is the most popular of the ontology languages (Kalibatiene & Vasilecas, 2011). There are less tools that support OWL than the other languages (Kalibatiene & Vasilecas, 2011), but it’s not about the quantity but rather the quality, and Protégé is only one tool, but well used. The popularity, the fact that is was designed for the semantic web, and that it was built on RDF and so retains some of it’s advantages, make it the best choice to express ontologies that can be utilised by software agents on the WWW. The choice of which variant is contextual though. I would suggest that OWL-Lite is not a good choice because it’s main advantage is ease of implementation, so learning a fuller version of OWL in order to take advantage of additional features would be sensible.

The choice between OWL-DL and OWL-Full is less clear. OWL-Full is “fully compatible with RDF, both syntactically and semantically” (Kalibatiene & Vasilecas, 2011: 128), which makes it the most expressive and the most compatible; good for mapping across domains. However, the high expressiveness comes at the cost of being undecidable with the loss of complete reasoning. OWL-DL is less expressive and is not fully compatible with RDF, but it still “corresponds with description logics” (Kalibatiene & Vasilecas, 2011: 128), and that might just be the sweet spot.

 

References
