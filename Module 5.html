Knowledge Representation and Reasoning

Studying the representation of the world that support programmes which can effectively represent the world around them.

This module aims to instil:

  1. The ability to define knowledge and explain the concept of KRR and its relationship to data and information.
  2. An appreciation of the importance and rationale for employing formal approaches to KRR.
  3. An understanding of the basics of logics and their applications to KRR problems.
  4. An understanding of the professional competence required to able be to identify and examine KRR technologies for Artificial Intelligence applications.

Learning Outcomes

  1. Critique the need for formal approaches to knowledge representation and reasoning.
  2. Review critically properties of a knowledge-based system.
  3. Appraise critically modelling techniques for knowledge representation and reasoning.
  4. Examine and incorporate different modelling approaches to solving KRR problems.

Reflections

Unit 1: Introduction to Knowledge Representation and Reasoning


I started this module with some trepidation because I believe it centres largely around First Order Logic, which was covered briefly in the Intelligent Agents module and I was not able to properly grasp the syntax. My hope is that if it's a core focus of this module we should spend more time on it for me to understand it better.

The initial lecturecast introduced KRR with defnitions of data, information and knowledge before describing KRR and ontologies. The topics were linked nicely, but at quite a high level so a lot more reading will be needed to understand this area.

The reading from the core book Brachman & Levesque (2004) continued the fairly simple introduction, defining (for the purposes of the book) knowledge, representation, reasoning, and how they come together with logic.

The other reading, Weststeijn (2011), was less useful. It was interesting to see views on how different languages inspired and influenced each other over the ages, but the quest for the primitive language (spoken by Adam) was futile at best, and driven by ulterior political and religious motives. Also, motives aside, it seemed to me to be more about tracing back languages to record information rather the represent knowledge.

Unit 2: Sets, Set Theory, Truth Tables and Logic


Unit 2 had no lecturecast so it was straight into reading. Partree et al (1993) started simply enough, with basic set theory; who doesn't recognise a Venn diagram? Chapter 1 described sets, subsets, unions and intersections. From chapter 2 it started to get a lot more complicated, covering ordered pairs (still not too difficult) relations (geeting harder), then fucntions, compositions, reflexivity, summetry, transistivity, connectedness, unbounded sets and infinite sets (actually the latter two topics were simpler than the rest). I understood most of it, but only when working through quite slowly, so if required to implement in practice anything other than the topics from chapter 1 I would need to work back through the text again. The other reading was Sharma & Pilling (2022) about constructing truth tables to calculate and illustrate logical relationships which I found to be relatively straightforward.

There was pre-work for the seminar this week covering selected exercises from chapter 1 of Partree et al (1993) plus the construction of some truth tables. I was able to check my answers in the book for the first exercise, with all answers being correct, but the second exercise didn't have answers supplied, and there are a couple of answers that I'm not confident about so it will be interesting to dissect in the seminar later in the week.
