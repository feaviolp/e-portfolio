Astrid van Toor

An interesting read Astrid. It’s clear that streaming services rely heavily on AI, which is probably why so many of us mentioned them in our own discussion posts.

You mentioned that techniques such as Collaborative filtering, used by Spotify, rely on longer term interactions and so new users don’t immediately benefit from them. I’m interested to know how Spotify and other streaming services therefore provide recommendations to brand new customers? Also, how quickly do they learn so the personalisation through Collaborative filtering kicks-in? Finally, is there an accepted timeframe, measured in elapsed time or perhaps number of interactions, within which a system should be sufficiently personalised in order to minimise the risk of the customer losing interest and moving elsewhere?

 

 

Hi Astrid,

Thanks for the details about the Heater method from Zhu et al (2021). It looks like an improvement in dealing with cold start issues, and seems to be especially useful in the case of cold user AND cold content (how to get content that is new to the system to right person who is also new to the system). I don’t think anyone is using the Heater method yet.

Burke (2002) examines five recommendation techniques including collaborative filtering and knowledge-based. The latter relies on content being catalogued, which overcomes the cold start issues but at the expense of being less accurate and presumably labour intensive to classify all new content, so a hybrid model is recommended using multiple methods, which actually aligns with Zhu et al (2020) saying that methods other their Heater method use auxiliary information.

Netflix tries to get around the cold user issue by asking new users what they like to watch to effectively “jump start the recommendation system” (Netflix, N.D.). They also have the “New on Netflix” carousel which I suppose helps to get around the cold content issue by making it available to everyone, and then as people start to interact, or not, with new content the collaborative filtering kicks-in.

References

Burke, R. (2002) Hybrid recommender systems: Survey and experiments. User Modeling and User-Adapted Interaction, 12(4): 331-370.

Netflix (N.D.) How Netflix’s Recommendations System Works. Available at: https://help.netflix.com/en/node/100639 [Accessed 3 October 2022].

Zhu, Z., Sefati, S., Saadatpanah, P. &  Caverlee, J. (2020) ‘Recommendation for New Users and New Items via Randomized Training and Mixture-of-Experts Transformation’, The 43rd International ACM SIGIR Conference on Research and Development in Information Retrieval. Virtual Event, China, 25-30 July:  Association for Computing Machinery. 1121-1130.

 

 

Dominic Lambert

Great opening statement from Stephen Hawking, Dominic. It really sets the tone.

I completely agree that AI is pervasive but often invisible, or rather so part of our daily lives that it isn’t noticed.

I don’t necessarily agree that Netflix provides biased recommendations because of viewing restrictions in different regions. I would argue that the content is simply not available in those regions, so the algorithm isn’t biased as such, it’s simply operating with a reduced choice. Possibly semantics, but I think not. That’s not to say that the recommendation system isn’t biased in other ways though, nor to say that having bias doesn’t have potentially serious consequences. I simply challenge that one use case.

News vs. fake news is another very interesting area. I agree with your findings, and would even go further to cite the deepfake video of president Zelensky that was posted to try to convince Ukrainian troops to surrender (Simonite, 2022), where the surprising part was how poorly executed the video was. I would also suggest that fake news is not just created by the obvious “bad actors”, but to some degree by all governments, keen as they are to have their view of the world accepted by their populations. I suspect that’s what you were driving at with your comment “despite there being an accord from 193 out of 199 countries we still have a problem”.

Samuel Adeniyi

Nice overview Sam.

The use cases that you’ve identified are all good examples of AI in practice, and it was good to call out the convergence with cloud processing as an enabler for mass adoption of AI

I don’t agree that “a company can avoid [bias] by ensuring ethical practice committees are in place as part of its AI processes”. Certainly the risk of bias can be mitigated, but I think it’s dangerous for any organisation, private or public, to convince itself that bias has been entirely avoided through the use of standards and processes. As you point out, any system based on data has the potential to develop bias, so we must remain vigilant to the risks and be ready to take action as needed when bias is detected. In other words a healthy mix of prevent and react.

Maksym Dudkin

You raise some good points about the use of AI in FinTech Max. It’s clear to see that AI is helping to reduce fraud and speed-up decisions. Do you see any downsides though? Perhaps any ethical challenges or financial risks? I would expect that an AI system that’s responsible for saving vast sums of money also has the potential to lose vast sums of money if it goes wrong. Or what about in the case of fraud prevention, if it gets a bit too protective and prevents legitimate transactions thinking they were fraudulent which resulted in a threat to someone’s safety because they couldn’t buy a train ticket to get home? As the power of AI increases it can be tempting to just ramp it up “because we can”. However, as the last example illustrates, we need to keep in mind the consequences, so we should be super careful about preventing large scale fraud, with a low risk tolerance for large values, but probably have a higher tolerance for lower values so we don’t stop someone getting a train ticket home in case an erroneous fraud flag gets triggered.

Rory Maclean

Thanks for an interesting read Rory.

I understand why you’ve challenged that AI is ubiquitous. I think it depends upon how literally we take the definition of ubiquitous. For me it means that something exists everywhere, or at least most places, in some form. Yes, “most places” does not strictly match the definition of “everywhere”, but I think when it comes to something that this question it will never be “everywhere”, so it’s more about being pervasive, or widespread. I say “in some form” because I believe that even immature applications of AI add to the ubiquity. I accept that you’ll disagree here.

Your life sciences examples of AI are very interesting, and of course topical regarding Covid-19. It’s a very valid and incredibly important point to make about the sharing of data to enable future research which benefits health. Of course pharmaceutical companies make obscene amounts of money by protecting their intellectual property, which is usually the result of significant investment, so it’s always going to be an ethical dilemma about how much to share for the benefit of humanity versus how much not to share to protect their own profits.
