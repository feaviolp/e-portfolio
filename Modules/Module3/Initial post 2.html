Initial Post

Greenland et al (2016) made some very interesting points about the mis-use of p-values and confidence intervals. The points were often quite subtle, and I needed to re-read the paper multiple times to grasp the finer details. In fact I suspect I still don’t fully understand all of the points raised, but I certainly have a much greater appreciation of the dangers of over-reliance on individual figures from statistical tests.

The p-value measures the compatibility between the observed data and a hypothesis and distils it into a single number. Issues raised in the paper include that there are other factors such as study protocols that can influence the p-value, and the p-value is over-used as a dichotomous tool rather than  a “continuous measure of compatibility”, which I found quite enlightening.

The issue to be discussed here though is “why is a p-value not always enough to report?”.

It is clear to me after reading the paper that reporting results using the p-value alone is not sufficient. Notwithstanding the other issues mentioned above, the p-value reports the compatibility of the data, but not the size of the effect. So a p-value on its own might suggest a highly statistically significant observation, but without knowing the size of the effect or the confidence interval we can’t see if it is meaningfully significant. For example, if we compared the recovery time from a particular illness with and without a particular drug and found that the drug gave a statistically significant reduction in recovery time, that sounds good, but on its own it is quite meaningless. If we add to that the mean recovery time of the group without the drug is 200 days, the mean recovery time of the group with the drug is 199 days, and the 95% confidence interval is 0.5 – 1.5 days it begins to look a lot less positive. The statistical significance remains, but it probably now lacks clinical significance. Bruce et al (2020: 108) agree, noting “by itself, a p-value does not provide a good measure of evidence regarding a model or hypothesis”. 

I suggest therefore, not that there are some circumstances where a p-value is not enough as per the original question, but rather that there are never any circumstances where a p-value on its own is enough.

The other question is “Under what situations should confidence intervals not be reported?”

Whilst there are similar challenges the confidence interval as p-value in the sense of not relying on it in isolation, I can’t think of any circumstances where using the confidence interval alongside other things like p-value and test score would be detrimental. So, much like the p-value, I would caution against using the confidence interval on its own, but I don’t think there are situations that it shouldn’t be used alongside other statistical measures.

References:

Greenland, S., Senn, SJ., Rothman, K. J., Carlin, B., Poole, C., Goodman, N., & Altman, G. (2016) Statistical tests, P values, confidence intervals, and power: a guide to misinterpretations. European Journal of Epidemiology 31(4). 337–350.

Bruce, P., Bruce, A. & Gedeck, P. (2020) Practical statistics for data scientists: 50+ essential concepts using R and Python. O'Reilly Media.
