Case Study: Accuracy of information - Summary Post

In my initial post I described what I consider to be fairly “by the book” responses to the questions raised. I believe that they are broadly correct, but left little room for debate. Raneli (2024a) was in broad agreement too.

I challenged Raneli (2024b) on one point; that whilst “underlining the ethical and legal ramifications of selective data representation” is correct in principle, in a real commercial setting it could be a little too confrontational for Abi’s employer’s liking. This highlights in a small way the tension between ethics and commercialism.

Lambert (2024) provided some alternative views to stimulate discussion. I disagreed that presenting information to promote a specific view is justifiable just because it is correct (Feaviour, 2024), although I conceded that this does happen (Fraser et al., 2018). I also disagreed that the question of ethics is unimportant because it’s just a cereal, although I suspect that was a tongue-in-cheek comment.

I did, however, agree that Abi should be concerned with the reputation of his employer as well as with good ethical practices. This once again highlights potential conflicts between ethics and profits, and whilst it would be nice to think that ethics will always be more important, there will be many situations where the opposition judgement is made, rightly or wrongly. In this case study, however, I maintain that Abi’s organisation’s reputation, and by extension it’s long term profitability, would be better served by acting ethically. And so in this case I believe that conflict would be easy to manage.
