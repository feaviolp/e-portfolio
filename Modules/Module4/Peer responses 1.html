Samuel Adeniyi

Hi Sam,

This is a good example of how a reputable company holding our most precious information (financial) can still be hacked. In this case the cost was to the company, but it can also be to the individual if their details are then used fraudulently.

We sometimes choose to deal with well-known brands because we believe they will be more secure, and treat our data more carefully, but they are not infallible as your research has shown.

Unfortunately customer data being stolen is not unusual. Andriole (2019) estimated that “in first half of 2018 alone, about 4.5 billion records were exposed as a result of data breaches”. Wikipedia (N.D.) lists data breaches since 2004. I know this isn’t a particularly good source, but the list of 370 different breaches containing some well-known, and dare I say trusted names, makes sobering reading. And there would likely be many additional breaches not reported!

References

Andriole, S. (2019) The Capital One Data Breach is No Exception & Why We Can Expect Many, Many More. Available from: https://www.forbes.com/sites/steveandriole/2019/07/30/the-capital-one-data-breach-is-no-exception-why-we-can-expect-many-many-more/?sh=677b32c0fc48 [Accessed 13 May 2023].

Wikipedia (N.D.) List of data breaches. Available from: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_data_breaches [Accessed 13 May 2023].

 

 

Astrid van Toor

Hi Astrid,

That’s a good example of the dangers of increasing digitisation. The ever increasing number of passwords needed to complete routine tasks online makes services such as LastPass sound appealing, but events like the issue that you described could cause significant brand damage to a company whose strapline includes “LastPass puts your digital life at your fingertips, simply and securely.”

To be fair to them, I commend their transparency in reporting the incident, as far as they were able. They also seemed to take robust measures beyond the minimum necessary, such as decommissioning and rebuilding the development environment rather than trying to find and fix the vulnerability (Toubba, 2022). That was probably necessary to limit the brand damage in light of their marketing as “safe hands” for customer data. I would, however, suggest that some of the remediation activities that they undertook should really have been done in the first place. Replacing the environment and developer laptops could of course only be retroactive. But the “additional logging and alerting capabilities” (Toubba, 2022) should perhaps have been there from the start. I appreciate hindsight is always 20:20, but with the use of (or more use of if they already use them) Ethical Hackers (Palmer, 2001) perhaps the issue could have been predicted and prevented?

References

Palmer, C. C. (2001) Ethical hacking. IBM Systems Journal, 40(3): 769-780.

Toubba, K. (2022) Notice of Recent Security Incident﻿. Available from: https://blog.lastpass.com/2022/12/notice-of-recent-security-incident/ [Accessed 13 May 2023].
