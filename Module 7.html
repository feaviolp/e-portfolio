Research Methods and Professional Practice

A practical foundation in research methods to enable research in practical settings.

This module aims to provide the ability to:

  1. Study and reflect on key principles and methods in research based on the scientific method and relevant to various disciplines.
  2. Examine various research strategies and designs as applicable to projects at hand.
  3. Develop research competencies, in particular those relating to the collection and analysis of data types to enable a critical design and evaluation of independent research.
  4. Take a reflective and independent approach to the learning process.

Learning Outcomes

  1. Appraise the professional, legal, social, cultural and ethical issues that affect computing professionals.
  2. Appraise the principles of academic investigation, applying them to a research topic in the applicable computing field.
  3. Evaluate critically existing literature, research design and methodology for the chosen topic, including data analysis processes.
  4. Produce and evaluate critically the resulting research proposal for the chosen topic.

Unit 6: Questionnaire Design


The lecturecast on questionnaires was very basic, covering things like the strengths and weaknesses of open and closed questions, and needing to ensure that questions are worded in such a way that they will be interpreted in the same way by people from different backgrounds. It also mentioned that questionnaires should be as short as possible with unnecessary questions omitted. Whilst I agree with that in principle, I reflected upon my case study reponse in unit 5, and the way that providing context in the form of background information or initial positioning questions can increase understanding, so there must be a balance and questionnaires shouldn't be cut down so much that they lose meaning, which I don't think is what was meant anyway.

The reading went into more detail about questionnaire tools, how to increase response rate, types of questions and the importance of the sequencing of questions, but it was still quite high level. There is additional reading available which I will explore if I choose to include a questionnaire in my final project.

There was a formative activity this week to find and critique a questionnaire on the wiki. I selected the Ofcom Media Attitudes Survey 2021 and found that the biggest issues were the differences in the sequence of the questions and possible answers between the online and paper versions of the same questionnaire, while the wording of the questions were fairly good:

  • Wiki Activity on Questionnaires

Unit 7: Validity and Generalisability in Research


This week the lecturecast concerned validity and generalisability in research, including population vs. sample, methods to remove bias, and differences between qualitative and quantitative research. Like last week the content was very basic, but I have come to value how the lecturecasts in a module help to guide the learning without going into too much detail.

The reading went into more detail about qualitative research, covering methods that I was broadly already familiar with. What was useful, however, were the examples of where qualitative research is useful, especially in combination with quantitative research such as when qualitiative research seeks to explain the reasons behind the findings of the quantitative research. The short video explaining confounding variables was also useful, showing with a simple example that a confounding varible is a variable other than those being studied that is the true cause of an effect. Not recognising confounding variables can lead to incorrect conclusions about cause and effect.

Unit 8: Inferential Statistics


The lecturecast and reading covered many of the statistical concepts previously covered in the Numerical Analysis module such as levels of measurement (ratio, interval, ordinal and nominal scales), discrete and continuous variables, the differences between and uses of mean, median and mode, skewness of data and hypothesis testing. There was not much new, but it was useful to be reminded of these methods prior to the beginning the final project where they might be needed, and some of the descriptions provided useful clarifications that I had not come across before, such as a nice visualisation of boxplots alongside skewed distributions. There were also some new concepts, such as the coeffecient of variation which I don't recall from the Numerical Analysis module.

The formative activities this week were some exercises in Excel to practice summary measures and hypothesis testing. I'm relatively proficient in Excel so the summary measures were straightforward, but I didn't realise that it was possible to calculate a t-test using the Data Analysis feature so that was very useful to learn:

  • Summary Measures
  • Hypothesis Testing
The seminar continued the discussion on statistical topics such as descriptive and inferential statistics, data types, central tendency and appropriate use of statistics. As with the reading it was largely a refresher of learning previously covered in the numerical analysis module, but it was still useful to run through this close to starting the final project. It was also very useful to get clarification that the final project does not necessarily have to include any statistical analysis at all. Rather than analysing primary and/or secondary data, if the project includes an artefact such as a machine learnig model, the performance measures of the model, such as accuracy, precision and f1-score could be used in place of statistical analysis of data.
