Deep Learning - Summary Post

In my initial post I outlined issues with generative AI including content ownership, accuracy, bias and opportunities for wilful misuse.

Adeniyi (2023) agreed, citing evidence of the threat of large scale misinformation campaigns (Mensier, 2023), and that Italy had previously banned the use of ChatGPT (McCallum, 2023).

Lukashevich (2023) also agreed with issues over content ownership, noting that Avrahami & Tamir (2021) have proposed potential candidates for ownership, including a machine learning solution to trace back ownership of generated images to the sources. None of the solutions are completely robust though, nor recognised in law. Which leads to another interesting point from Lukashevich (2023); that the United Kingdom is the only country that recognises computer generated works within law (Lucchi, 2023). I knew that the UK was pushing to be a leader in artificial intelligence, but I did not realise that it had taken those steps.

I added to other posts about the need for guardrails to ensure generative AI doesn’t become biased, like Microsoft’s chatbot “Tay” that was quickly withdrawn because it became racist and sexist (Schwartz, 2019). There are, however, difficulties in how to apply guardrails. Since large language models are trained on data, one way would be curating the data, but when does that become censorship? And similar if we control the output – who gets to say what is acceptable and what is not? There are obvious extremes, but who defines the hard line in the grey middle?

Finally, van Toor (2023) provided an interesting angle on the impact of generated art on artists who have own their own style. It made me think a lot more about the potential negative impacts on the creative arts from generative AI. It drew comparisons for me with recent music copyright cases (Feaviour, 2023).
