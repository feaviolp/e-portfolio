Astrid van Toor

Hi Astrid,

It's interesting that you state that "SVMs can be reliably trained with relatively little data". My research didn't find the same, and I believe Bell (2020) only suggested taking a small sub-set of the data for training because there was such a large volume of data with a simple structure in the first place (100k rows with only 4 attributes including only 4 output values). I note that you are comparing SVM to ANN though and I'd certainly agree that ANN requires much more training data than SVM so if you meant relative to each other then I agree.

Continuing on training data, I found sources saying that SVM suffered from excessive computational cost when dealing with large data sets (Cervantes et al, 2020; Ray, 2019), which might be another reason to reduce the size of the data set; out of necessity? Conversely Osisanwo (2017) found that SVM performed better with larger data sets, so I suspect it's like most things in life - finding the right balance is actually what's needed rather than an absolute "more is best" or "less is best"...

Dominic Lambert

Hi Dominic,

Wow, that’s a long and thorough initial post.

Picking one small part on which to provide feedback, I found your statement that “supervised learning is more commonly used in scenarios that are resultant of greater consequence, or instances that have greater possibility to be to our detriment” interesting and prompted a little research of my own. It seems that most studies comparing machine learning algorithms focus on supervised or unsupervised, with few comparing between them (Alloghani et al, 2020). Digging a little deeper with some real examples I discovered that your hypothesis is broadly correct though. For example, Suresh and Guttag (2021) assess the potential causes of harm in machine learning and it’s fair to say that most apply more to supervised than unsupervised learning. That’s not to say that unsupervised learning is safe, of course there are still risks, but with a bit of research I find myself agreeing with you that supervised learning, by virtue of its typical applications and that it relies upon human generated labels, caries more risk of harm than unsupervised learning.

Rory Maclean

Hi Rory,

My interest was piqued when you said that NB can be used to generate new text examples. I did a bit of research and found a simple example that made sense to me, although the results were poor in the example because it was not using text, but rather it was categorising images by features but then generating new images using different combinations of the same features (so far so good) but it rendered with pixels which is where it fell down because of the independence of variables, in this case pixels, in NB (Gupta, 2019).

In any case, back to the text use case. Now that I understand how it could work, my question is, where do you think it would be useful? Since it uses the same probabilities of words appearing in any given category, it follows (to me at least) that any generated text will have roughly the same set of words in the same ratios as other texts already used in the learning phase. So, it would provide no meaningful change to the output of the algorithm. In fact, it would be counter-productive if it did since the synthetic data would be skewing the results in an unpredictable way. I therefore assume that the generated text is not to be used for further training of the algorithm, which leaves using the generated text for some other purpose. Going back to the principle that NB considers all variables to be independent, I can’t see how any generated text could be constructed into sentences, it would just be a bag of words in appropriate ratios for the categorisation, so it would be difficult to read or make sense of any generated text. Am I missing something?

Anastasia Rizzo

Hi Dominic, Astrid,

I didn’t expect any of the discussions about specific machine learning algorithms to venture into artificial general intelligence (AGI), but it’s an interesting area so I’ll add my views.

Firstly, to deal with the initial comments about a universally applicable algorithm, I believe that this can be taken in two ways. Firstly, the way I would have initially interpreted it had it not been for the follow-up discussions involving deities etc., would be to stay within the realm of narrow AI, where the algorithm is trying to answer a specific question. In this case, whilst I acknowledge that different algorithms seem to perform better on certain data sets than others, and hence we have a choice of algorithms in the supervised and unsupervised kit boxes, I do believe that it’s reasonable to consider that as the algorithms become more sophisticated that one day there will be a “winner” in each category (supervised and unsupervised) that will always perform best on any data set, and striving for such perfection isn’t a waste of time and resources. Indeed it’s challenges like that which ensures that humans continue to innovate. However (there’s always a “but”), there are other considerations over and above just giving the best results. Transparency and explainability still remain very important, especially in industries such as financial services as Max explained (Dudkin, 2022). So even if we did find the perfect algorithm, it’s likely that we’d still need alternatives to meet other ethical and regulatory requirements.

Now, to the AGI discussion that we unexpectedly (to me at least) strayed into. Here my view is somewhere in-between the two of you. I believe that we will probably never truly achieve AGI. Fjelland (2020) highlights a key challenge in that computers do not exist “in our world” because they cannot comprehend the wider context of the environment that they are in. It could be argued that as data becomes ever more vast, computational power faster and cheaper, and sensors more sophisticated, that one day computers will overcome this limitation, but today they don’t even look at the world the same way; AI algorithms are interested in correlation, not causation (Fjelland, 2020). A further quote that really rings true for me is “Big data is about what, not why” (Mayer-Schönberger & Cukier, 2014: 14). Having said that, Miller and Bostrom (2016) surveyed experts in AI and keeping aside selection bias for the purpose of this discussion reply, found that “experts think that superintelligence is likely to come in a few decades and quite possibly bad for humanity”, although they themselves concede that AI predictions are rarely accurate.

So to split the difference between your two opinions, I’m not convinced that we will achieve AGI, but I do still believe that we should strive for it. Firstly because as humans we innovate when we stretch towards seemingly impossible goals (even falling short of the ultimate goal would no doubt advance other goals). Secondly, whether we think we should strive for it or not, I’m sure that others will, and IF it’s ever achieved there could be dire consequences for the human race, so better to keep the topic in full view so we can try to ensure that appropriate checks and balances are put in place to maximise the benefit and minimise the harm if AGI ever becomes a reality.
