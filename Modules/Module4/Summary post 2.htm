Legal and Ethical views on ANN applications - Summary Post

In my initial post I discussed how education will need to change to adapt to a world with generative AI such as ChatGPT, but that the change is probably overdue anyway, so ChatGPT has just accelerated the need (Heaven, 2023). I also described how I believe that work will become more efficient with sensible use of generative AI.

Van Toor (2023) agreed that the means of assessing students will need to change to avoid cheating with generative AI, but did also agree that tools such as ChatGPT could also be used as an educational tool, like a calculator in a maths exam. Finding the balance is certainly going to be a challenge in the short term. I agree that we need to think immediately about how we can fairly assess students when they have access to tools like ChatGPT, but longer term I strongly believe that modernising the education system to use such tools (in moderation) to prepare people for work in a world where they will inevitably be part of daily life has got to be the way to go.

Adeniyi (2023a) noted that Italy had previously banned the use of ChatGPT due to difficulties in verifying who was using it, meaning that minors might enter sensitive information. This is an example of the need for regulation, but also the difficulty in regulation. So it clearly isnâ€™t going to be a simple path.

Adeniyi (2023b) also mentioned the issue of plagiarism with generative AI due to the models being trained on data scraped off the Internet. In response (Feaviour, 2023) I noted that Lindau (2023) found issues with ownership of generated content, risks that generated content could infringe upon patents, and even the inability to patent computer generated content in the US.

There is still much to learn.
