The 4th Industrial Revolution - Initial Post

Schwab (2017) describes the 4th industrial revolution, noting the exponential pace not seen in previous industrial revolutions. He describes some of the impacts on people, business and economies and a selection of key technologies pushing the technological advancement. Crucially, he notes that as well as bringing positive changes facilitated by big data and the increasing compute power to process it, there are many risks too.

A real-life scenario where an information system failed with catastrophic consequences is the Boeing 737 MAX aircraft, or more specifically it’s manoeuvring characteristics augmentation system (MCAS).

Boeing was under pressure from its competitor Airbus to introduce a new, more fuel efficient plane. Instead of developing a new plane it adapted it’s existing 737 plane with bigger, more fuel efficient engines (Herket et al 2020). The larger engines had to be positioned higher on the wings, which resulted in different handling characteristics. This led to an increased stall risk, but rather than training pilots to deal with it, which would cost time and money, the MCAS was developed to override pilot control in certain circumstances to keep the nose down and prevent stalling (Johnston and Harris, 2019).

Two key mistakes made in the deployment of MCAS were:

1.    It’s installation was not documented, so pilots were unaware of the additional “help” being offered, and therefore how to react if it was in fact unhelpful.

2.    It operated off a single angle of attack sensor, which could fail or give an inaccurate reading.

Two tragic accidents occurred involving Boeing 737 MAX aircraft, in October 2018 and March 2019, where everyone on board was killed with a total of 346 lives lost. The MCAS was considered to have given a significant contribution to the both crashes (Johnston and Harris, 2019).

The impact was huge. In addition to 346 people being tragically killed, the financial cost to Boeing in lost revenue (the planes were grounded in most countries and orders were cancelled), compensation and brand damage was estimated at $19bn (Rushe, 2020).

The failings that led to the crashes were not software bugs, they were design choices. Boeing wanted to deliver a better plane faster than it would take to design a new plane and have it certified by the FAA, so they adapted an existing plane and tried to mask unintended handling consequences with the MCAS. They then intentionally failed to disclose the MCAS to pilots to keep up the pretence that it was simply an updated 737 and handled as such (Travis 2019).
