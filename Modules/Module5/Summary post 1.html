Knowledge Representation is a recent phenomenon - Summary Post

In my initial post I asserted that the term knowledge representation was recent, but the endeavour to represent knowledge was not, citing examples of Venn (1881), Boole (1854) and Aristotle (lived 384-322 BC). I also stated that I believed that knowledge representation on it’s own is useful, but reasoning makes it more so.

Van Toor (2023a) initially concurred that reasoning adds value, however after reading other posts she changed her mind and said that “KR can be equally useful without reasoning support” (Van Toor, 2023b). She used a map as an example of a representation of geographical knowledge that requires no additional reasoning.

I challenged that a map represents knowledge, suggesting instead that it “holds information” (Feaviour, 2023a). I similarly challenged Lambert (2023) with the difference between information and knowledge (Feaviour, 2023b). However, after continuing the discussion around the map example with Maclean (2023a), I changed my opinion. I now agree that a map is in fact the representation of knowledge (Feaviour, 2023c). However, I believe that once committed to an artefact (the map) it then becomes information. Unlike van Toor (2023b), I still maintain that reasoning can extract additional value from the map though. By supplementing with information such as weather conditions, a person or system could reason with the information (stored knowledge) in the map and the weather to draw conclusions about when/where it is safe to go.

This brings us to another discussion which was about the definition of reasoning. I initially said that reasoning makes knowledge actionable when it is incomplete (Feaviour, 2023d). Maclean (2023b) disagreed, saying that the outcomes doesn’t need to be actionable, and perhaps “a new or updated belief is a more useful output”. I conceded that saying the outcome of reasoning needs to be actionable was perhaps overstating it, however “updating a belief or creating a new belief [could both be considered] actions” so the definition still seems reasonable (Feaviour, 2023e).
