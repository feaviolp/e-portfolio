Knowledge Representation and Reasoning

Studying the representation of the world that support programmes which can effectively represent the world around them.

This module aims to instil:

  1. The ability to define knowledge and explain the concept of KRR and its relationship to data and information.
  2. An appreciation of the importance and rationale for employing formal approaches to KRR.
  3. An understanding of the basics of logics and their applications to KRR problems.
  4. An understanding of the professional competence required to able be to identify and examine KRR technologies for Artificial Intelligence applications.

Learning Outcomes

  1. Critique the need for formal approaches to knowledge representation and reasoning.
  2. Review critically properties of a knowledge-based system.
  3. Appraise critically modelling techniques for knowledge representation and reasoning.
  4. Examine and incorporate different modelling approaches to solving KRR problems.

Reflections

Unit 1: Introduction to Knowledge Representation and Reasoning


I start this module with some trepidation because I believe it centres largely around First Order Logic, which was covered briefly in the Intelligent Agents module and I was not able to properly grasp the syntax. My hope is that if it's a core focus of this module we should spend more time on it for me to understand it better.
The lecturecast introduced KRR with defnitions of data, information and knowledge before describing KRR and ontologies. The topics were linked nicely, but at quite a high level so a lot more reading will be needed to understand this area.
The reading from the core book Brachman & Levesque (2004) continued the fairly simple introduction, defining (for the purposes of the book) knowledge, representation, reasoning, and how the come together with logic.
The other reading, Weststeijn (2011), was less useful. It was interesting to see views on how different languages inspired and influenced each other over the ages, but the quest for the primitive language (spoken by Adam) was futile at best, and driven by ulterior political and religious motives. Motives aside, it seemed to me to be more about tracing back languages to record information rather the represent knowledge.
