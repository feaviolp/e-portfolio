Codes of Ethics and Professional Conduct - Initial Post

Case: Malicious Inputs to Content Filters

The Blocker Plus case (Association for Computing Machinery, N.D.) has good intentions, but fails to comply with certain ethical standards. The case will be assessed against ACM Code of Ethics and Professional Conduct (Association for Computing Machinery, 2018), referred to as “ACM”, and the Code of Conduct for BCS Members (British Computer Society, 2022), referred to as “BCS”. The assessment is divided into legal, professional and social issues.

Legal issues

“Blocker Plus is complying with U.S. federal regulations to facilitate socially responsible uses of computers” (Association for Computing Machinery, N.D.). There is no mention in the case study of Blocker Plus being used outside of the United States of America, so there don’t appear to be any issues pertaining to other jurisdictions, although care should be taken if there were plans to distribute it more widely to ensure compliance with local jurisdictions. In this regard it is compliant with ACM principle 2.3 “Know and respect existing rules pertaining to professional work” (where “rules” can be read as “laws”), and with BCS 3.a “carry out your professional responsibilities with due care and diligence in accordance with the Relevant Authority’s requirements whilst exercising your professional judgement at all times”

Professional issues

Blocker Plus failed to “give comprehensive and thorough evaluations of computer systems and their impacts, including analysis of possible risks” (ACM principle 2.5) and “design and implement systems that are robustly and usably secure” (ACM principle 2.9) because they didn’t foresee and protect against misuse by activist groups, which ultimately corrupted the classification model. The closest BCS clause to ACM principle 2.5 appears to be 2.e “respect and value alternative viewpoints and, seek, accept and offer honest criticisms of work”, however at first blush it would appear that Blocker Plus is not in breach of that clause on the basis of not foreseeing the risk of misuse. I suggest that Blocker Plus still failed BCS 2.e on the basis of not addressing the issue of model corruption when it was first identified because “there was no easy way to correct the model” (Association for Computing Machinery, N.D.), but the failing is for a different reason. The closest BCS clause to ACM principle 2.9 appears to be 2.a “only undertake to do work or provide a service that is within your professional competence” and whilst in hindsight there were clearly issues with the product, I find it difficult to argue that professional competence could have been called into question during development.

Social Issues

Blocker Plus complies with ACM principle 1.1 “Contribute to society and to human well-being, acknowledging that all people are stakeholders in computing” because it seeks to filter content for children, as designed, but to have the capability to disable the filters for adults as required. The is similar to BCS 1.d “promote equal access to the benefits of IT and seek to promote the inclusion of all sectors in society wherever opportunities arise.”

Due to the issues with the corrupted model incorrectly blocking legitimate information, Blocker Plus fails principles 1.2 “Avoid harm” and 1.4 “Be fair and take action not to discriminate”, whilst failing BCS 1.a “have due regard for public health, privacy, security and wellbeing of others and the environment” and 1.c “conduct your professional activities without discrimination on the grounds of sex, sexual orientation, marital status, nationality, colour, race, ethnic origin, religion, age or disability, or of any other condition or requirement”.

ACM and BCS Comparison

There are many similarities between ACM and BCS. Both provide ethical frameworks for systems and software development, but they are not identical, with BCS having some gaps, at least as far as this case study is concerned. ACM is also much more detail, with significantly richer descriptions.
