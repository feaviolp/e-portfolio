Intelligent Agents

A study of software systems which perceive their environment and act in that environment in pursuit of their goals.

Learning Outcomes

  1. Identify and critically analyse agent-based systems, differentiating between architectures and approaches.
  2. Apply and critically evaluate intelligent agent techniques to real-world problems, particularly where technical risk and uncertainty is involved.
  3. Deploy critically appropriate software tools and skills for the design and implementation of an agent-based system, bearing in mind applicable legal, social, ethical, and professional issues.
  4. Systematically develop and implement the skills required to be effective member of a development team in a virtual professional environment, adopting real-life perspectives on team roles and organisation.

Unit 5: Agent Communication


This lecturecast was very different to others on the course so far, starting with a discussion about the semantics of speech before briefly covering the agent based communication language KQML, which will be covered in more detail in later reading. Frustratingly, once again the quiz required an incorrect answer on order to achieve 100%.

The reading this week was extremely verbose. Searle (1969) went to great lengths to effectively decompose langage into "speech acts" which are individual tokens of information that vary depending upon context. The simplicity of my summary compared to the length of the book serves to illustrate the challenge, in that the book attempts to codify natural language, which relies upon a general understanding of contexct and meaning between the person speaking and the person being spoken to. It turns out that codifying it is incredibly complicated!

This is brought back to the topic of agents with Payne and Tamma (2014) who propose a method for agents to negotiate with each other when their ontologies and goals differ. This is something that I hadn't really thought about before, thinking that agents simply pass requests and responses back and forth, but in a true multi-agent system where agents are independent with different goals, a way to negotiate as well as just send instructions and responses does seem necessary. Food for thought here.

Unfortunately Anastasia decided to leave our project group this week for personal reasons and join another group. We are almost ready to submit the project and it's been almost entirely Astrid's and my work to date anyway so this shouldn't pose any problems for us.

Finally, another collaborative discussion started this week about the advantages and disadvantages of the agent communication language (ACL) Knowledge Query and Manipulation language (KQML). My initial post is here:

Unit 8: Understanding Natural Language Processing (NLP)


The seminar this week was about Natural Language Processing (NLP). Unit 12: The Future of Intelligent Agents


Unit 12 looked at the future of intelligent agents. The reading was lighter in this final unit, which was welcome because the individual reflection also had to be written this week and I hadn't started it early due to completing the team project in unit 11. It was still a useful read, about artificial intelligence incidents and ethics. Much was reinforcement of what had already been covered, but one comment about autonomous drone swarms being a weapon of mass destruction struck a chord with me given the current war in Ukraine and the way that both Russia and Ukraine are adapting their weapons for strategic advantage, as well as due to supply issues of conventional weapons.

The final seminar covered the e-portfolio and individual reflection, and as per the reading, the future of intelligent agents. I have been keeping my e-portfolio updated each week so that part is practically done, but I am struggling a little with the individual reflection because it's not the way that I typically think. I am starting it now. The "discussion" on the future of intelligent agents (not really a discussion because unfortunately no-one was able to atend in person so I listened to the recording) was short but thought provoking. We already know that AI, and by extension intelligent agents, are a hot topic in the media at the moment. However, two things really stuck out for me in the seminar; that work is underway to tune AI responses to make them feel more trustworky and make people feel more comfortable with them, and the idea of intelligent agents becoming lifeling companions through continuous learning. I took from the first point that it was important for AI to FEEL more trustworrth, not necessarily to BE more trustworth, which leading into AI becoming a lifeling companion starts to bring to mind thoughts of distopian science fiction. I realise that the lifelong companion can simply be a Netflix recommender that knows what I want to watch before I do, but the direction of travel is still one to be very mindful of. I still believe that AI, including intelligent agents, can be a force for good, However there are many eithical risks that remain, with bad actors always waiting to take advantage. The industry must remain vigilant.
