The genie is well and truly out of the bottle! Large language models have been around for some time, but in November 2022 GPT-3 (ChatGPT) made it easily accessible to the public, and it quickly went viral (Marr, 2023). The combination of the power, the broad scope, and the ease of use has made it the topic of conversation from technical conferences to family dinners. ChatGPT is becoming to generative AI what Google is to the search engine.

There has been a lot of discussion and speculation about the potential negative impact on education, such as using ChatGPT to pass exams (Shearing & McCallum, 2023) and coursework (Acres, 2023). Those discussions, however, assume that the teaching syllabus and assessment methods remain unchanged. As the world, and especially technology, develops around us, shouldn’t we prepare our children by adapting their education to meet their changing needs? Heaven (2023) assessed the likely impact of ChatGPT on education and found that it should have a broadly positive impact, but it will take some effort on the part of education institutions. Two quotes that resonated with me from that article are “In a system with too much focus on grading and not enough on learning, ChatGPT is forcing a debate that is overdue” (Helen Crompton, associate professor of instructional technology) and “Did ChatGPT kill assessments? They were probably already dead, and they’ve been in zombie mode for a long time. What ChatGPT did was call us out on that” (Richard Culatta, CEO of the International Society for Technology in Education (ISTE)). The fact does remain though, that in the short-term ChatGPT can be used to cheat so education institutions will need to take steps to guard against that while looking at how to adapt and modernise the entire approach to education in the future.

Sticking with education, Kasneci et al (2023) believes that large language models provide opportunities to create educational content, improving student engagement with personalised learning experiences. They note challenges too, such as the need to fact-check the generated content, and risks of bias, but those are not unique to generative AI. In fact, if the bulk of the content can be generated automatically, at a higher and more personalised standard that before, that leaves more time to polish the materials including fact and bias checking. So the key is augmenting, not replacing, the teacher.

It's a similar story in the world of work. Large language models such as ChatGPT can be used to accelerate the work of people. Microsoft is integrating large language models into it’s Microsoft 365 suite to provide natural language prompts based upon a user’s data, such as highlighting important activities and summarising multiple emails and chats (Spataro, 2023). Also, ChatGPT can be used to generate draft content that can be edited and used, similar to the education use case above. However, care should be taken because ChatGPT is still new, and OpenAI give no liability for damages arising from it’s use. They also use the input and output data to develop and improve the model “which places the risk on the user to ensure that such transfer to OpenAI is permitted under data protection law” (Raven, 2023). So putting personal or commercially sensitive data into ChatGPT should be avoided, but huge value can still be extracted by using abstracted data, and with cloud computing bigger organisations can build their own large language models with their data safely contained.

I attended the AI Summit in London two weeks ago and a quote that I remember, although I can’t remember who said it, was “people shouldn’t be afraid of generative AI taking their jobs, they should be afraid of people who can use generative AI better than they can taking their jobs”. I believe that’s true. Generative AI is providing a new toolkit for people to use, and a new set of skills (prompt engineering) to use it. People who learn to use the tool effectively, including understanding it’s challenges and limitations such as accuracy and bias and navigating around them, will succeed. And this brings me back to education. Let’s not fear generative AI. Let’s embrace it, use it to enrich the education of the next generation, and especially make sure that people are educated to be able to maximise the value from it whilst appreciating it’s limitations.
