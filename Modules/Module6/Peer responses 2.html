Alberto Castro

Hi Alberto,

You gave a good summary. The only point I’d like to challenge is “distributed systems (using the object-oriented paradigm) can also handle the points above”. I confess that I’ve been having a hard time differentiating agents from components in a distributed system, and in some cases I think they could be the same thing. But I believe in purist cases when agents are entirely independent, each with their own goal, that is quite different to a distributed system where there are multiple distributed components but as a “system” they share common goals. I agree that in such a system, components written in different languages could communicate via APIs, but it’s still a transactional communication. Whereas in a true multi-agent system there needs to be a means of agents not just sending and receiving, but negotiating, and that, I believe is where the additional data pertaining to goals in ALCs comes in – effectively giving the agent a reason to cooperate.

That said, it’s still a little confusing so I’m happy to be challenged.

 

 

Vasilisa Lukashevich

Hi Vasilisa,

My reply to you is along similar lines to my reply to Alberto (Feaviour, 2023). This does raise the prospect that it’s me who has it wrong, but I’ll go ahead anyway.

Your description of Amazon, which was very good by the way, sounds more like a distributed system than a multi-agent system. The components would be communicating using APIs etc., but they would be transactional communications such as “take payment x from credit card y”. Whilst I agree that could be both a distributed system and a multi-agent system, it doesn’t necessarily have the features needed to necessitate using an ACL, which is where each agent has its own goal(s) and so needs to be passed a bit more information in order for it contribute to the overall system goals through the achievement of its own. As I said though, I might not have it quite right.
