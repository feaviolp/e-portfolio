Astrid van Toor

Hi Astrid,

Once again we find ourselves broadly in agreement.

This discussion has made me think about what we mean by knowledge representation. Before the module started I had assumed it was about codifying knowledge with, for example, First Order Logic. However, the question posed about the origins of knowledge representation uncovered things that I had previously taken for granted and didn’t automatically associate with the subject. In my own initial post, Feaviour (2023), I mentioned Venn diagrams (Venn, 1881), Boolean algebra (Boole, 1854) and, like you Aristotle. Upon reading Balke & Mainzer (2005) from your initial post I can add tree diagrams being used by Raimundus Lullus in the 13th century, long before Charles Darwin illustrated the tree of life (Darwin, 1859).

I realise that this doesn’t add anything to the specific discussions about whether knowledge representation is new (we both agree that it isn’t) or whether it is useful without reasoning (we both agree that it is, albeit less so). I just found it amusing to think about how pervasive some of the tools are such that I didn’t consciously think of them as being for knowledge management. If you asked me to name a tool for information storage my first answer is unlikely to be a pen either!

Samuel Adeniyi

Hi Sam,

We agree that knowledge representation is not a recent phenomenon, and that knowledge representation alone is useful without reasoning, but reasoning increases its usefulness.

I don’t necessarily agree that Weststeijn (2011) provides a good account of the history of human endeavours in knowledge representation. I found that, whilst it was interesting, most of the findings were more concerned with information storage than true knowledge representation, and the efforts to trace back to the “primitive language” were driven by political and religious motives, not to mention being futile. Perhaps my own personal views were influencing how I interpreted it though, and I certainly agree that it had a broad historical scope. In fact one thing from that paper which you mentioned and I omitted is knowledge represented in art.

Astrid van Toor in reply to Samuel Adeniyi

Hi Astrid,

It’s good to question our own reasoning as well as that of others. However, in this case I believe that we had it right the first time.

In your initial post you said “KR is still useful without reasoning since it can still represent relevant information, but that KR is less powerful without this support” (Van Toor, 2023a). In Sam’s initial post he said “Although KR can still be useful without reasoning support, its capabilities are limited” (Adeniyi, 2023). In my initial post I said “knowledge representation on its own does indeed have value, but reasoning elevates it to a higher level” (Feaviour, 2023). So we all agreed that knowledge representation has value on its own, but it can be enhanced with the addition of reasoning. We didn’t quantify how much value knowledge representation has without reasoning, and I would agree that it can be a lot. But that doesn’t contradict our initial assertions that reasoning adds even more value. I do note that in your follow-up you said "KR can be equally useful without reasoning support " (Van Toor ,2023b) so it looks like you have indeed changed your position, which is fair enough, but for now I stand behind my original assertion that however valuable knowledge representation is, reasoning will always elevate it to a higher value.

I would also challenge that a map represents knowledge. I would say that it holds information, and when put into the hands of an experienced hiker who is perhaps familiar with local weather conditions, sunrise/sunset times, appropriate hiking equipment etc., it could contribute to knowledge of when/where to safely hike. Those additional pieces of information held by the experienced hiker could also be stored somewhere, as separate pieces of information, and when combined with logical relationships they could be considered to be a knowledge base, or represented knowledge, which would be more useful than the map on its own to a novice hiker who doesn’t already have that knowledge. But if we have a hiker’s sat nav app that recommends a route based upon the experience level of the hiker, current weather, recent weather, time of day etc., or perhaps even recommends not to hike at all due to dangerous conditions, now you’ve hit the jackpot with reasoning.

All of that is based upon my interpretations of course so I’m open to be challenged. Especially on the differences between information and knowledge because I found that it was quite easy to define them conceptually, as many sources have, but it became much more difficult to find the dividing line in practice when working through the map example above.

Rory Maclean in reply to Samuel Adeniyi, Astrid van Toor and Leigh Feaviour

Hi Rory,

I agree that we could debate the differences between data, information and knowledge until the end of time and possibly never come to a hard, unambiguous  definition. So perhaps continuing the debate is futile, but it’s fun so here we go…

I agree that the difficulty arises when it comes to representing knowledge. Defining information and knowledge seems to be more straightforward, but then when we talk about representing knowledge we start to run into trouble. So let’s go back to simply defining them:

Ackoff (1989) seems to be heavily cited in papers discussing data, information, knowledge and wisdom. He says:

“Data are symbols that represent properties of objects, events and their environment”

“Information is contained in descriptions, answers to questions that begin with such words as who, what, where, when and how many.”

“Knowledge is know-how, for example, how a system works. It is what makes possible the transformation of information into instructions.”

Based upon those definitions I would still say that a map information. The map maker certainly had knowledge when they produced it, but what is stored in the pages of the map is information. A user with knowledge of how to read a map can use the information contained to answer questions such as “how far”, “how steep” etc., but to do that the use has to have knowledge of how to read a map.

So the question then is, how then can we represent the knowledge of the map maker? The answer would seem to be to encode his knowledge into a map, which is precisely what is done in an ordnance survey may that represents gradients etc. So now I agree with your and Astrid’s assertion that a map is in fact a representation of knowledge. The difficulty for me was the difference between “what is knowledge” and “how to represent knowledge”. I therefore propose a distinction that says “knowledge can be represented in an artefact, but once contained within that artefact it is, in and of itself, information”, or more simply “knowledge can be represented as information”. I think this deals with your assertion that a map is a representation of knowledge irrespective of who picks it up, but it also deals with my assertion that it can only be useful to someone who already has the knowledge of how to read it, because having been encoded into the map is has become information, needing knowledge to decode. What do you think?

 

References

Ackoff, R.L. (1989) From data to wisdom. Journal of applied systems analysis, 16(1), pp.3-9.
