Codes of Ethics and Professional Conduct - Peer Responses

Dominic Lambert

Hi Dominic,

I enjoyed reading your post, with your insights from having worked in the defence industry. It’s easy to take case studies such as this at face value, but your critique backed by real-world experience was insightful and thought provoking.

I agree with you that the engineers were culpable, potentially criminally, by breaking non-disclosure agreements. Having a conflict of ethics with Q Industries and deciding to resign as a result is fine, but breaching the non-disclosure agreement is not. Particularly disturbing from the case study: “the engineers were right to foster public awareness of these risks, and breaking their confidentiality agreements would be ethically justified.” (Association for Computing Machinery, N.D.). Ethically justified perhaps, but it’s a dangerous statement to say that they were “right” without evidence of unlawful behaviour, of which there is none.

However, returning to the exercise, it is clear that Q Industries were in breach of many ACM principles, and given the nature of their business, that must be a concern. I accept that they have not, as far as we can see, broken any laws. I also accept that ACM and BCS carry no legislative weight. But I do still think that the assessment against the ACM principles is a worthwhile exercise to bring to life some of the principles. To your own point about the lack of sufficient legislation for ethical violations, would it not be the case that assessing compliance against non-binding codes of conduct at least sets a benchmark, albeit unenforceable, which is a start?

References

Association for Computing Machinery (N.D.) Case: Automated Active Response Weaponry. Available from: https://ethics.acm.org/code-of-ethics/using-the-code/case-automated-active-response-weaponry/ [Accessed 17 November 2023].

 

Alberto Castro

Hi Alberto,

I think we can all agree with your conclusion that “dark UX is a bad practice that violates several ethical principles”. What I find interesting about this particular case study, however, is the accountability of the client versus the development agency versus the developer. The client is clearly being unethical with their request. In fact the level of bad practice is almost comically bad in order to make the point, and you highlight some in your post. However, the case study analysis suggests that it is the developer wo is at fault, such as “computing professionals have a moral obligation to use their skills to benefit the members of society (Principle 1.1)” (Association for Computing Machinery, N.D.). I agree that in this case the developer clearly contravened that principle, and we have seen that they already had reservations and raised them to his manager (does that make him more ethical or more complicit?).

My question is, should the development agency be held accountable for failing to adhere to the principles? I believe so, especially in light of the concerns being raised from within their own organisation. Then, should the developer, Simon, similarly be held accountable? According to the principles the answer is clearly “yes”, and from your post I think you feel the same, but in the real world I can understand a developer smelling that something isn’t right, raising it this manager, being told to do it anyway, and so getting on and doing as he is told. It’s a job after all, and one that is paying his bills. Controversial perhaps, but something to ponder….

Dominic Lambert in reply to Leigh Feaviour

Hi Dominic,

Thanks for your reply. I agree that the discussion is more interesting when focused on the points of disagreement.

You make a valid and timely point that ACM and BCS lack “legislative teeth” in that they are codes of conduct that are not legally binding. I started by stating that Blocker Plus is compliant with US federal regulations, and much of the rest of my post detailed failures to comply with ACM and BCS, so it could be assumed that the difference is implied, but I completely agree that I was not clear.

On the point about jurisdiction, however, I would still say that the case study only made reference to compliance with US federal regulation. So ignoring ACM and BCS for a moment, I maintain that they would need to assess local compliance and potentially make changes in order to comply with local regulations before deploying to other regions.

Your second point about the presumption of fault after the fact is interesting. I agree that no system is completely secure, and your reference was a good read. I also agree that it would be unhelpful to assume that a system was not designed robustly and securely simply because it has been compromised. However, I disagree with your view that there was no evidence to suggest that measures were inadequate. ACM principle 2.9 says “Computing professionals should perform due diligence to ensure the system functions as intended, and take appropriate action to secure resources against accidental and intentional misuse, modification, and denial of service. As threats can arise and change after a system is deployed, computing professionals should integrate mitigation techniques and policies, such as monitoring, patching, and vulnerability reporting. Computing professionals should also take steps to ensure parties affected by data breaches are notified in a timely and clear manner, providing appropriate guidance and remediation.” (Association for Computing Machinery, 2018). Anyone developing a content filter would surely be aware of the risk of bad actors trying to compromise is, so whilst it’s not possible to prevent an attack, they should have been aware of the likelihood, and if not they would surely be contravening principle 2.6 “Perform work only in areas of competence.” So on the basis that they were aware of the risks, they should have included mitigation techniques and policies to deal with them. On the contrary, they had no easy way to correct the model, suggesting that mitigation had not been considered during design, and they failed to notify their stakeholders about the problem. Based upon those two points, my view remains that Blocker Plus failed to adhere to principle ACM 2.9. But we agree that the ramifications are reputational and not legal.

References

Association for Computing Machinery (2018) ACM Code of Ethics and Professional Conduct. Available from: https://www.acm.org/code-of-ethics [Accessed 17 November 2023].
