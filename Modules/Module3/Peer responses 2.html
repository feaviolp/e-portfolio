Once again we have very similar views so I'm not sure of the value in just agreeing with what you've said. Rather than doing that, I'll instead introduce another topic from the paper for your opinions:

I was interested in the view from the paper that the p-value is over-used as a dichotomous tool rather than  a “continuous measure of compatibility”. I have previously felt that by setting an alpha value and then making a significant/not significant statement based upon which side the p-value falls, we can potentially miss some of the subtleties that the data is telling us if the actual p-value was very close to the alpha value. I understand the need to be binary for results to be declared significant or not, otherwise where would we draw the line? What would stop people moving the line to support their own needs from the study? Indeed, that's one reason why the alpha value must be set before the study results are known. But the dichotomous nature of this approach sometimes misses important information. We declare "not significant" when alpha is 0.05 and p-value is 0.051 but with just a slightly larger sample size the result could easily have gone the other way. It's tricky in my mind so I thought I'd see if you had any thoughts on the subject?
