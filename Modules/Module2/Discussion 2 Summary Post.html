Summary Post

In my initial post I selected supervised learning algorithms Decision Tree (DT) and Support Vector Machine (SVM). Three other people selected DT (Adeniyi, 2022; Dudkin, 2022a; Lambert, 2022a) and four selected SVM (Dudkin, 2022a; Lambert, 2022; Maclean, 2022a; van Toor, 2022a).

Lambert (2022a) highlighted that supervised learning is typically used where the consequences are greater than unsupervised. After some research, including Suresh & Guttag (2021), I agreed (Feaviour, 2022a).

My assertion that a key strength of DT is its readability was supported by Adeniyi (2022) and Dudkin (2022a).

Van Toor (2022b) noted in reply to Adeniyi (2022) that DT is a learning method and not an algorithm; it’s the specific variant of DT, such as ID3, that is the algorithm. I concede that this is technically correct, but for the purpose of the discussion, colloquially referring to DT as an algorithm is acceptable.

I described SVM as a binary classification algorithm, but Dudkin (2022b) and Maclean (2022b) disagreed. I concur that SVM can be used in a multi-class solution, but this is achieved by stacking multiple SVM classifications, so I maintain that the SVM algorithm itself is still binary (Feaviour, 2022b; 2022c). It’s an interesting debate that can be viewed both ways.

Dudkin (2022a) said that SVM could not be used in financial services because of it’s black-box nature. I suggested that there could be acceptable scenarios where SVM makes a less consequential decision, validated by a human (Feaviour, 2022d). He initially disagreed (Dudkin, 2022c) but later agreed (Dudkin, 2022d).

Finally, I enjoyed the unexpected discussion about Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) where van Toor (2022c) believes it’s futile whilst Lambert (2022b; 2022c) believes it will probably be possible in the future. I believe that it probably won’t happen, but if it does there are significant risks to humanity (Feaviour, 2022e).
