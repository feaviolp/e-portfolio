Case Study: Accuracy of information - Peer Responses

Dominic Lambert

Hi Dominic,

I was a little concerned that this discussion would end up with everyone saying very similar things and not being much of a “discussion”. So thanks for providing some points that I can disagree with 😊

I disagree that presenting information that promotes Whizzz more favourably is justifiable just because it is correct. That’s not to say this behaviour isn’t prevalent, as identified by Fraser et al. (2018) and cited in my initial post (Feaviour, 2024). Sadly this is probably one reason why the phrase “lies, damned lies, and statistics” is still so widely used. Suggesting that Abi might advise Whizz to request the information in a specific way in order to get the results that they want leans further into this unethical (in my opinion) behaviour.

I agree that Abi must have concern for the reputation of the organisation that he works for, so he needs to be careful about what he reports, and how bluntly. But I suggest that the reputation of his organisation would be better served by providing ethically sounds results rather than misrepresenting, albeit positively, this one client. On that basis I do agree with your view that Abi should provide all of the information, and there are limits as to his accountability in what they then do with it.

I confess to having a chuckle at your final paragraph; “who cares?”. I understand the sentiment, and I expect it was a little tongue-in-cheek, but unsurprisingly, when taken at face value I disagree. The brief said that the data might suggest that the cereal is potentially harmful. That may, or may not, fall short of killing someone, but whatever the severity of the harm, it would be in the public interest to know it. Even if it’s as simple as being nutritionally questionable, given the challenge of growing obesity (Baker, 2023) it is still important to present accurate and complete findings, not just cherry pick the positives. Which is why the questions should be agreed before the research, not created after to match the best results.

In any case, I enjoyed reading and responding to your post.

References

Baker, C. (2023) Research Briefing: Obesity statistics. Available from: https://researchbriefings.files.parliament.uk/documents/SN03336/SN03336.pdf [Accessed 18th January 2024].

Giuseppe Raneli

Hi Giuseppe,

I agree with most of what you have said so I won’t waste time re-stating it.

The one area where we differ slightly is your suggestion that Abi should “communicate his findings to the manufacturer, underlining the ethical and legal ramifications of selective data representation”. The principle is fine, but in the real work, where Abi is employed by an organisation and the manufacturer is their client, I think they might take a dim view of such overly blunt, almost accusational messages. The disclaimer in the report seems like a good idea though, and would serve the same purpose, so personally I would suggest stopping there.
