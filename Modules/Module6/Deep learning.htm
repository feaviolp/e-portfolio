Deep Learning

There are currently many applications of deep learning. Narasimman (2023) has helpfully compiled a list, including self-driving cars, virtual assistants, fraud detection, image colouring and advertising, to name a few. Interesting they don’t mention generative AI such as GPT which seems like a strange omission given the recent publicity. In any case, for my example of a deep learning application I have chosen to find something that has not yet been discussed in order to bring a fresh perspective. To that end I have chosen to look at breast cancer diagnosis using deep learning. This isn’t a completely new topic, but it hasn’t been discussed so far in this module.

Medical diagnosis by human experts can be significantly improved with the use of deep learning (Azad et al, 2021). There have been many recent experiments to diagnose skin cancer using convolutional neural networks (CNNs). Haggenmüller et al (2021) examined 19 such studies and found that all of the CNN models gave superior or equivalent performance to trained clinicians. There is a caveat, however, that most of the studies were conducted using a single image for each patient. Clinicians performed better when given multiple images and additional information such as patient age and sex, and the location of the lesion. It still looks very encouraging though. I expect a solution incorporating the additional details that improve clinician accuracy could also be used to improve the deep learning solution accuracy too. For example, the CNN could be trained not to provide a binary malignant or benign output, but rather some weighted outputs that could then go into another algorithm such as an artificial neural network (ANN), or even a decision tree, where the additional data such as age, gender and lesion location could be included to calculate the final diagnoses.

Telemedicine, whereby patients consult with clinicians via audio-visual media on their smartphones, and specifically teledermatology focusing on early diagnosis of skin cancer, is already growing in popularity due to its time saving for both the patient and the clinician (Fabbrocini, 2011). It seems a natural step, therefore, to take the same smartphone but send the pictures to a CNN for initial diagnosis, referring to a clinician where necessary. Studies are already underway in this area (Azad, 2021).

That all sounds good, but there are of course ethical considerations. Firstly, deep learning models rely on large volumes of data on which to be trained. Medical data is, but it’s nature, sensitive, so patient confidentiality must be considered when training the models.

Next there is the question of accuracy. If an advertising algorithm gets it wrong, you might see an advert that doesn’t interest you. If a medical diagnosis algorithm gets it wrong you could be mis-diagnosed with potentially lethal consequences. The stakes are high, so accuracy is always a hot topic. However, clinicians don’t always get it right either. So is accuracy equivalent or better than a clinician all that is needed? Perhaps, but what if you are one who is mis—diagnosed by the deep learning algorithm? You would be quite justified in asking if the same mistake would have been made by a clinician. Averages and statistics are fine, but with it’s you personally then it becomes binary; right or wrong. It’s a difficult conundrum.

I do believe, however, that this use case of early detection and referral for potential skin cancer could be a very good example. It could be a self-service tool for people who have noticed an unusual lesion to get an instant answer to the question “should I be worried?”. The algorithm could be trained to be err on the side of caution, referring even marginal cases to an actual clinician, so the clinician still saves time by not seeing the people who are obviously clear, but they still have the final say on everyone else, at least while the accuracy continues to improve until it can potentially be allowed to “run solo”.

In the current climate of overworked healthcare staff and the availability of smartphones to practically everyone, personally I think this is an excellent use of deep learning, provided it is implemented carefully, with patient health taking precedence over clinical efficiency.

 

References
