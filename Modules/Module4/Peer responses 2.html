Legal and Ethical views on ANN applications - Peer Responses

Astrid van Toor

Hi Astrid,

I too have been giving generative AI a lot of thought recently. It’s amazing how quickly it’s become mainstream with so many people talking about it and using it, even if “just for fun”. I can’t think of another technology that has taken-off so quickly.

There is certainly concern about how the education system will need to adapt to stop students from being able to cheat using tools like ChatGPT. You referenced Burrows (2023) who gave some sensible tactics to prevent mis-use in the short term. However, I believe that it goes further than that. Even before ChatGPT it could be argued that our education system was in need of modernisation. I discussed in my initial post (Feaviour, 2023) the thinking that ChatGPT could be the catalyst that forces a complete re-think about how students are taught and assessed (Heaven, 2023). This isn’t just about “getting around” generative AI, but about modernising the entire approach to education to better meet the needs of today. I believe that generative AI will become ever more part of working life. Microsoft is already integrating it into its office suit (Spataro, 2023), so we should be teaching future generations how to use it, including its pitfalls and when not to use it. I remember being at school and questioning why calculators weren’t allowed in maths exams, because ”in the real world” they would be available. I can see the same parallel with ChatGPT.

I agree with your comments about the creative industry. Augmenting administrative tasks to make people more efficient is one thing, but the ability to automatically generate a novel or an image is a worry. I’m not sure what the answer is for this.

Samuel Adeniyi

Hi Sam,

I found the plagiarism angle in your post particularly interesting.  Lindau (2023) discussed ownership of ChatGPT generated content and it’s fair to say that more work needs to be done to properly understand and legislate for generated content. For example, I agree that content is generated based upon the content that it was trained on. So on one hand you might say that the model should go back and determine all of the inputs and their respective owners to properly credit the generated output. However, I would argue that generated output is itself new and so credit is not needed. Much like people are influenced by many things when they come up with “new” content, and they would never be able to recall all of those influences.

Another pitfall noted by Lindau (2023) is if generated content inadvertently infringes on a patent, for example generated code. Liability is questionable here.

Speaking of patents, the same article also notes that in the US, something can only be patented or copyrighted if it was created by a human. So if something has been generated by AI, but based upon human prompts and subsequently modified by a human, can that be patented or copyrighted? It seems that still needs to be tested.

It’s going to be very interesting to see how the legal system deals with this complex subject. I even wonder if the legal system itself is fit for purpose in this context, or if a more radical overhaul is needed.

 

References

Lindau, B. (2023) ChatGPT: Who Owns the Content Generated? Available at: https://www.jdsupra.com/legalnews/chatgpt-who-owns-the-content-generated-2891692 [Accessed 01 July 2023].
