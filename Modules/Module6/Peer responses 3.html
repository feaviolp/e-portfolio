Deep Learning - Peer Responses

Astrid van Toor

Hi Astrid,

You observations about unwilling use of data in the form of artistic images for the generation of “new” art really peaked my interest. As you said, I discussed the issue of copyright ownership on generated content in my initial post, but for me that was about who would have ownership rights of generated content assuming it was original. What I hadn’t considered was the rights over content used as inputs to the models. It’s a seemingly obvious omission, but I think I was considering that the output, even if influenced by (trained on) other data, would still be considered original. Your post has given me a different perspective on that. Reading your links about how systems can be asked to generate art “in the style of” is very worrying for the creative industry.

It puts me in mind of music copyright cases recently, where music only has to have a passing resemblance to something that preceded it and there’s the risk of a copyright case, as shown by recent high profile cases in MI Library (2022). Whilst I think that in some cases the musical resemblance is tenuous at best, I can fully appreciate why artists are incensed by images being generated in their unique style without their permission.

 

References

MI Library (2022) Recent Cases. Available from: https://library.mi.edu/musiccopyright/currentcases [Accessed 06 July 2023].

 

 

Samuel Adeniyi

Hi Sam, Astrid,

The debate about ethics and legislation concerning misinformation, harmful language, biases and deepfakes got me thinking about how far we should go to provide safe guardrails without straying into unnecessary censorship.

I think we can agree that guardrails should be provided to ensure AI does not become biased, such as Amazons aborted recruitment tool (Dastin, 2018) or hateful like Microsoft’s chatbot “Tay” that was withdrawn after 2 days because it learned to become racist and sexist (Schwartz, 2019). The issue is how to put in appropriate guardrails without creating unnecessary censorship. Large language models are trained on data, so we could curate the data in order to make the output more palatable, but who decides what to include/exclude? The same is true if we put the controls on the output to prevent certain outcomes. Some might be obvious like “don’t be sexist, racist or extremist” but once someone has control, they are able to bend it to their own will, even unwittingly. How extreme is “extremist”? Everyone has views, and a consequence of being human is we all think we’re right. So whoever is implementing the controls will naturally tend towards their own beliefs, and that itself could lead to social engineering. So let’s let the governments and legislative bodies decide on the rules, right? Well I don’t think that even needs to be answered… The US supreme may even grant first amendment rights of freedom of speech to generative AI (Amon, 2023) !

Serious food for thought here.

 

References

Amon, A. (2023) Rights and Regulation: The Future of Generative AI Under the First Amendment. Available from: https://www.skynettoday.com/overviews/gen-ai-first-amendment [Accessed 07 July 2023].

Dastin, J. (2018) Amazon scraps secret AI recruiting tool that showed bias against women. Available from: https://www.reuters.com/article/us-amazon-com-jobs-automation-insight-idUSKCN1MK08G [Accessed 07 July 2023].

Schwartz, O. (2019) In 2016, Microsoft’s Racist Chatbot Revealed the Dangers of Online Conversation The bot learned language from people on Twitter—but it also learned values. Available from: https://spectrum.ieee.org/in-2016-microsofts-racist-chatbot-revealed-the-dangers-of-online-conversation [Accessed 07 July 2023].
