Deep Learning - Initial Post

Generative AI such as ChatGPT for text and Dall-E for images offer unprecedented opportunities, but they come with ethical issues that need to be considered.

One such issue is content ownership. Lindau (2023) discusses various issues of ownership of the content generated by ChatGPT. The content generated is based upon a large language model that was trained on a huge amount of data, some of which was no doubt copyright material. If the output is derived from that copyright material, should the original authors be credited? Or given the amount of different data sources being used should we consider that the original sources merely serve as “inspiration” and were not directly copied? Or if we decide that a copyright infringement has occurred, who is liable? OpenAI  makes it clear that the user is responsible for content (OpenAI, 2023), but the user doesn’t know which sources were used to generate the content. Now, what if the content is in fact new and unique and the user wants to protect it, because it was generated based upon their prompts so it’s their content, right? The US Copyright Office recently published guidance to say that content generated by AI, without material modifications by a human, cannot be copyrighted.

Accuracy is another issue. Text and image generators will spew out seemingly confident answers to prompts, but they are not always accurate. There could be inaccuracies in the training data, and in the complex pattern matching to produce the output. For example, Kang et al (2023) note issues with accuracy and reproducibility of maps using DALL-E.

Bias is a potential issue for all AI models, and generative AI is no exception. “Ask Dall-E for a nurse, and it will produce women. Ask it for a lawyer, it will produce men. A “restaurant” will be western; a “wedding” will be heterosexual” (Hearn 2022).

Finally for this post (there are many more examples that I won’t cover here) is wilful misuse of content. There are already bad actors trying to do harm, and generative AI makes it easier for them. Hacking is made easier by creating fake data for phishing scams, fake documents to commit fraud, and rapidly generate new strains of malware to keep ahead of detection tools (Biniyaz, 2023).
