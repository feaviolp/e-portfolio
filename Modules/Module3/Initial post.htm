Initial Post

The table is large and difficult to read as a wall of text and numbers. There are a number of ways of addressing the issue.

The first thing considered was to simplify the statements to make the less wordy. For example "I believe that I am sufficiently competent in obstetrics to be able to offer intrapartum care to my patients" could be simplified to "Competence to offer intrapartum care". There is a risk that the meaning behind the statements that the GPs answered could be lost or misinterpreted, however, so it was decided to leave the statements in their full form.

It was thought that the best way to make the table easier to read was to use a graphical format. A stacked bar chart looked like the best way to present the data. After briefly exploring barplot in R, it quickly became obvious that something more sophisticated would be needed so ggplot was used instead.

I used a lot of trial and error to finally get to a chart that presented the data in a horizontal format with the bars filled (all at 100%), showing the count of each response, sequencing the fill as Agree -> No Opinion -> Disagree to clearly how level of agreement from left to right. Showing the count was important to retain that data from the table, and because not everyone answered every question, so total responses for each question varied.

I also had to edit the format of the CSV file to get the chart to plot correctly. I think this is down to my limited understanding of ggplot, even with a lot of Internet searches for help, so with more time I expect I could make it work in the original file format. I also had to prefix each question with a letter to get it to sequence the statements in descending order of Agree counts, which with more experience I think should be possible within ggplot without having to use this untidy workaround.

I could have left the chart set to 'stack' rather than 'fill' to show the differences in responses rates, but that made the chart look a bit untidy. I also wanted to sequence the statements in order of most agree to least agree to make it easier to read down (or up) the chart.
