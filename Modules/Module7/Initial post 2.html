Case Study: Accuracy of information - Initial Post

Abi is a researcher at an institute and also a statistical programmer. Abi has received a project from a manufacturer to review the nutritional value of a new cereal, Whizzz. Having collected the necessary data, he now needs to perform the appropriate analyses and print the reports for him to send to the manufacturer. Unfortunately, the data Abi has collected seems to refute the claim that Whizzz is nutritious, and, in fact, they may indicate that Whizzz is harmful.

Abi also realises that some other correlations could be performed that would cast Whizzz in a more favourable light. “After all,” he thinks, “I can use statistics to support either side of any issue.”

Ethical Concerns

  • Clearly, if Abi changed data values in this study he would be acting unethically. But is it any more ethical for him to suggest analysing correct data in a way that supports two or more different conclusions?
  • Is Abi obligated to present both the positive and the negative analyses?
  • Is Abi responsible for the use to which others put his program results?
  • If Abi does put forward both sets of results to the manufacturer, he suspects that they will publicise only the positive ones. What other courses of action has he?

You should also highlight legal, social and professional impacts of any choices made.

 

It would certainly be unethical for Abi to analyse and present data in a way designed to support a specific conclusion. The House of Commons has published a research briefing which covers various inappropriate practices, one of which being selectivity as to “formulate [an] argument or conclusion first, then cherry pick data to support and ‘explain’ this” (Bolton, 2023: 8). This would appear to meet the criteria of selectivity, and so would clearly be unethical. Instead, Abi should formulate the hypothesis first, and then objectively determine if the data supports that hypothesis or not.

Similarly, in order to be ethical and objective, Abi is required to present his findings in full, both positive and negative (and indeed where no statistical significance has been found). Again, these should be based upon the original hypotheses from before the data was seen, not generated afterwards.

Abi cannot be held entirely accountable for how his results are used, but he is responsible for providing clear and transparent results to reduce the likelihood, as far as practicable, that they can be misused.

Abi should report his results fully and transparently, including both sets of results if they are both derived satisfactorily from the original hypotheses. If the results are misused in some way there is then a trail back to the original results that can be used to objectively find the facts and not the “spin”.  If Abi is concerned that the manufacturer will cherry pick pieces of his report to create false conclusions, he should take care to ensure that the report does not contain ambiguous or misleading statements that could be used in isolation to draw incorrect conclusions.

By adhering to these principles, Abi might not be popular with the manufacturer, but he will maintain ethical and legal standards whilst upholding the integrity of scientific research. This will help to protect his reputation and career, which is more important than this one job.

Worryingly, it seems that whilst the ethical path for Abi is clear, there is much scope to act unethically. Fraser et al (2018) surveyed 807 researchers and found that over half admitted to at least once engaging in questionable research practices!

 

References

Bolton, P. (2023) Research Briefing: How to spot spin and inappropriate use of statistics. London: House of Commons Library.

Fraser, H., Parker, T., Nakagawa, S., Barnett, A. & Fidler, F. (2018) Questionable research practices in ecology and evolution. PLOS One, 13(7), 1-16.
